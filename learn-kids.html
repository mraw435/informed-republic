🏛️ The Three Branches of Government

The United States government is split into three parts, called branches. Each branch has a different job, and they all keep an eye on each other so that no single person or group gets too much power. This is called checks and balances.

📜

Legislative Branch

Congress — the Senate and the House of Representatives. They make the laws.

🦅

Executive Branch

The President and the Cabinet. They carry out the laws and run the country day to day.

⚖️

Judicial Branch

The Supreme Court and other courts. They decide what the laws mean and whether they're fair.

Why three branches? The Founders of the United States didn't want any one person to have all the power — like a king. So they split the government into three parts that each watch over the others. If one branch tries to do something wrong, the others can stop it!

💡

Fun fact! The United States Capitol building in Washington D.C. — where Congress meets — has 541 rooms, 850 doorways, and 300 skylights. It took over 30 years to build!

Lesson 2 of 4

📜 How a Bill Becomes a Law

A law is a rule that everyone in the country must follow. But before something becomes a law, it starts as an idea — and it has to go on a long journey!

  1. Someone has an idea. A member of Congress — a Senator or a Representative — comes up with an idea for a new law. This could be anyone's idea: a student, a teacher, a business owner, or even you!
  2. The idea becomes a bill. The idea is written down as a bill — a formal document that explains the new law.
  3. A committee studies it. A small group of Congress members (called a committee) reads the bill carefully and decides if it's a good idea.
  4. The full Congress votes. First the House of Representatives votes. If it passes, the Senate votes. Both must say yes!
  5. The President decides. If Congress says yes, the bill goes to the President. The President can sign it (and it becomes a law!) or veto it (say no).
  6. It becomes a law! Once signed, the bill is now a law that everyone must follow.
💡

Fun fact! Most bills never become laws! Out of thousands of bills introduced in Congress each year, only a small number make it all the way through the process.

Lesson 3 of 4

🗳️ How Elections Work

In the United States, citizens vote to choose their leaders. This is what makes America a democracy — the people decide who is in charge!

Who can vote? United States citizens who are 18 years old or older can vote. That means someday, you'll be able to help choose the President, your Senators, and your Representative!

The biggest election in the United States is the Presidential Election, held every four years. Here's how it works:

  1. People from each political party vote in primary elections to pick who they want as their candidate.
  2. Each party picks their candidate at a big meeting called a convention.
  3. The candidates travel around the country, giving speeches and debating each other.
  4. On Election Day (the first Tuesday after November 1st), citizens vote!
  5. The winner is determined by the Electoral College — a special system where each state has a certain number of votes based on its population.
💡

Fun fact! The youngest person ever elected President was John F. Kennedy, who was 43 years old. You have to be at least 35 years old to run for President!

Lesson 4 of 4

📋 What Is the Constitution?

The Constitution is the most important document in the United States. Think of it as the rulebook for the entire government. It was written in 1787 — more than 235 years ago — and it still guides our country today!

The Constitution does three big things:

  1. It creates the three branches of government and explains what each one can do.
  2. It protects your rights. The first ten additions to the Constitution are called the Bill of Rights, and they guarantee things like freedom of speech, freedom of religion, and the right to a fair trial.
  3. It sets the rules for how new laws can be made and how the Constitution itself can be changed (called an amendment).

The Bill of Rights — The first ten amendments to the Constitution protect the rights of every American. They include the right to say what you think, the right to practice any religion, and the right to a fair trial if you're ever accused of something wrong.

💡

Fun fact! The original Constitution is kept at the National Archives building in Washington D.C. The document is stored in a special case filled with argon gas to keep it from deteriorating — you can go see it in person!
